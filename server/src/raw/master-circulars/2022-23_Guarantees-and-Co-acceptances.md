**Original Issue Date:** 01 April 2025
**Updated As On:** Not specified
**Applicable To:** All Scheduled Commercial Banks (excluding Payment Banks and RRBs)
**Regulatory Topic:** Credit
**Supersedes:** Master Circular DOR.STR.REC.2/13.07.010/2024-25 dated April 01, 2024

## Summary

This Master Circular provides a consolidated framework for banks regarding the conduct of guarantee and co-acceptance business. It outlines prudential norms for financial and performance guarantees, maturity limits, and unsecured exposures. Key mandates include the requirement for Board-approved policies for non-fund based facilities, strict margin requirements for brokers (50%), and the absolute obligation to honor invoked guarantees without delay or demur. It also prohibits guarantees for the placement of funds with NBFCs and sets out rigorous internal control systems to prevent frauds and ensure staff accountability.

## Sections

### 1. Introduction
An important criterion for judging the soundness of a banking institution is the size and character of its contingent liability commitments. Banks execute performance and financial guarantees structured by security, maturity, and purpose.

### 2. Guidelines

#### 2.1 General Guidelines
2.1.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks should confine themselves to the provision of financial guarantees and exercise due caution with regard to performance guarantee business.
2.1.2 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks should guarantee shorter maturities and leave longer maturities to other institutions.
2.1.3 ⚠️ Obligation: No bank guarantee should normally have a maturity of more than 10 years. Banks may evolve a policy for longer durations with Board approval, considering ALM impact.
2.1.4 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks should generally refrain from issuing non-fund based facilities to constituents who do not enjoy credit facilities with them. Granting such facilities to those with no fund-based debt in India requires a comprehensive Board-approved policy and the same appraisal level as fund-based debt.
2.1.5 ⚠️ Obligation: BG/LCs issued against counter-guarantees of co-operative banks require banks to satisfy themselves of the co-operative bank's KYC regime and monitoring systems.

#### 2.2 Guidelines relating to conduct of guarantee business
2.2.1 Norms for unsecured advances & guarantees
2.2.1.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks' Boards have the freedom to fix their own policies on unsecured exposures. "Unsecured exposure" is defined as an exposure where the realisable value of security is not more than 10 per cent, ab-initio.
2.2.2 Precautions for issuing guarantees
⚠️ Obligation: Banks should avoid undue concentration of unsecured guarantees to particular groups/trades. Guarantees for individuals should bear a reasonable proportion to the constituent's equity.
2.2.3 Precautions for averting frauds
⚠️ Obligation: For performance guarantees, banks must ensure the customer has the necessary experience and capacity to perform the contract to avoid default.
2.2.5 Internal control systems
⚠️ Obligation: Bank guarantees for Rs. 50,000 and above should be signed by two officials jointly.
2.2.6 Guarantees on behalf of Banks' Directors
2.2.6.2 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks must ensure adequate arrangements are made so that commitments are met by the director's own resources and the bank is not called upon to grant a loan to meet the guarantee invocation.
2.2.8 Guarantees on behalf of Share and Stock Brokers/ Commodity Brokers
⚠️ Obligation: Banks must obtain a minimum margin of 50 percent (with minimum 25 percent cash margin) for such guarantees.
2.2.9 Personal guarantees of promoters/directors
⚠️ Obligation: Personal guarantees should be taken only when absolutely warranted. No consideration (commission/brokerage) shall be paid by the company to the directors for providing these guarantees.

#### 2.3 Other Stipulations – Issuing Bid Bonds and Performance Guarantees for export
Banks should adopt a flexible approach to boost exports.
2.3.1.1 ⚠️ Obligation: For unconditional guarantees favoring overseas employers, banks must obtain an undertaking from the exporter that the bank is entitled to pay upon invocation notwithstanding any dispute.

#### 2.4 Restrictions on guarantees for placement of funds with NBFCs or other non-bank entities
2.4.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks should not execute guarantees for enabling placement of funds with NBFCs or other non-banking entities directly or indirectly, including inter-company deposits.
2.4.3.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks issuing guarantees favoring other lending agencies must ensure:
(i) A Board-approved policy is in place.
(ii) The guarantee is only for borrower constituents.
(iii) The guaranteeing bank assumes a funded exposure of at least 10%.
(vi) ⚠️ Obligation: Banks are prohibited from providing guarantees for the issuance of bonds or debt instruments of any kind. They may only provide Partial Credit Enhancement (PCE) in the form of a non-funded irrevocable contingent line of credit.

#### 2.5 Payment of invoked guarantees
2.5.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Payment should be made to beneficiaries without delay and demur. No delay is permitted on the pretext of seeking legal advice.
2.5.8 (i) ⚠️ Obligation: Guarantees must be honored without hesitation unless there is a Court order restraining the bank.
2.5.8 (iv) ⚠️ Obligation: For any non-payment in time, staff accountability should be fixed and stern disciplinary action (including dismissal) taken.
18 (a) ⚠️ Obligation: Pension paying banks should compensate pensioners for delay in crediting revised pension at a fixed interest rate of 8 per cent per annum.

#### 2.6 Co-acceptance of bills
2.6.2 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks should co-accept only genuine trade bills where goods are actually received.
2.6.2 (xiii) ⚠️ Obligation: Co-acceptances for Rs. 10,000 and above must be signed by two officials jointly.
2.6.2.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks are precluded from co-accepting bills drawn by NBFCs.

#### 2.7 Precautions in the case of Letters of Credit
2.7.1 ⚠️ Obligation: Banks must be vigilant in import LCs; payments should be released only after ensuring shipping documents strictly conform to LC terms.

## Definitions

- **Unsecured Exposure:** An exposure where the realisable value of the security, as assessed by the bank/approved valuers, is not more than 10 per cent, ab-initio, of the outstanding exposure.
- **Security:** Tangible security properly charged to the bank; excludes intangible securities like guarantees or letters of comfort.
- **Relative:** Includes members of a Hindu Undivided Family, husband, wife, and specific relations like father, mother, son, daughter, brother, sister (including step-relations and spouses).
- **Major Shareholder:** A person holding 10 per cent or more of the paid-up share capital.
